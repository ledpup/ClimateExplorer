# Methane, Or: Review of Hot Talk, Cold Science, part 1

*Hot Talk, Cold Science*, by [Fred Singer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fred_Singer), is a climate sceptic book that questions the science of climate change. The first scientific claim in the book is from Seitz’ forward. It’s not specifically about climate change; it's about methane in the atmosphere.

> [Singer] predicted that methane, once it reached the stratosphere, would be transformed into water vapor, leading to a possible depletion of stratospheric ozone.

Scientists would not normally say something like "methane (CH4) transforms into water (H2O)" because carbon can not simply disappear in a chemical reaction (nor does oxygen simply appear). What Seitz probably meant that is that methane, in the straosphere, would undergo a chemical reaction that resulted in a product that included water vapour.

## Atmospheric layers

There are five layers in the atmosphere. Seitz started the Forward by discussing a reaction that occurs in the second layer, the stratosphere. Methane starts in the troposhere (often originating underground, perhaps during coal or crude oil mining, or by natural processes).

![Atmosphere layers]({{site.url}}/blog/assets/atmosphere-layers.png)

Methane leaves the troposhere and stratosphere in similar ways, usually by reacting with an hydroxyl radical - a molecule that has the formular ·OH. The dot signifies that it's a [free radical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radical_(chemistry)), something that will easily undergo a chemical reaction.

The formular for the reaction of methane with hydroxyl is:

<p style="text-align: center;">CH4 + ·OH → CH3· + H2O</p>
